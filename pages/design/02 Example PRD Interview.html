The Interview

Site Coordinator Hub — Product Discovery

A 30-minute discovery session with a product manager and a lead site coordinator. This interview was conducted before any design work began — the kind of brief a designer might actually receive. It has the detail and the gaps of a real session.

Product Discovery Interview · Site Coordinator Hub · v1 · March 12, 2026
ProjectSite Coordinator Workflow Dashboard — v1
InterviewerSarah Chen, UX Design
AttendeesMarcus Webb (PM) · Brittany Osei (Lead Site Coordinator)
ObjectiveUnderstand the coordinator's current workflow and define v1 scope
Sarah — UX Designer (interviewer)
Brittany — Lead Site Coordinator (end user)
Marcus — Product Manager
Part 1 — Current workflow
Sarah
UX Design
Thanks for making time. Before we get into specifics — Brittany, can you walk me through what a typical morning looks like? What are you doing the moment you sit down?
Brittany
Coordinator
The first thing I do is check email. Honestly, that's where everything lives right now. I'm looking for anything from patients about cancelling or rescheduling, anything from the sponsor about protocol updates, and if I have a monitoring visit coming up I'm trying to figure out if everything's in order.
Sarah
UX Design
What does "in order" mean for a monitoring visit?
Brittany
Coordinator
A CRA is going to come in and review source documents against what's in the system. I need to make sure every patient who's due for a data entry review actually has their data entered, and any flags from the last visit are resolved. Right now I track all of that in a spreadsheet. It's not — it's not great.
Marcus
Product
That spreadsheet is exactly what we're trying to replace. The goal is to give Brittany one place where she can see which patients need attention, what's coming up in the next two weeks, and what's already overdue.
Part 2 — Patient management
Sarah
UX Design
When you say "patients who need attention" — what does that actually mean? What puts a patient into that category?
Brittany
Coordinator
It could be a lot of things. A visit window that's closing — they have a protocol visit due within a certain timeframe and they're getting close to the deadline. Or a patient we tried to contact and couldn't reach. Or lab results that came back flagged and need a clinician to review them. Or a protocol deviation we need to document.
Sarah
UX Design
Those sound like very different levels of urgency. Are they weighted differently in your mind?
Brittany
Coordinator
Yes, definitely. A closing visit window is urgent in a way that an unreviewed lab result might not be. But right now everything is mixed together in email and I can't really triage.
Marcus
Product
We're thinking about a priority system — red for urgent, amber for follow-up needed, green for on track. We haven't fully worked out what triggers each level yet.
Sarah
UX Design
How many patients would a coordinator typically be managing at once?
Brittany
Coordinator
Depends on the study. Anywhere from 10 to 40 patients across one or two protocols, sometimes three if they're smaller studies.
Sarah
UX Design
What do you need to see at a glance for each patient?
Brittany
Coordinator
Their ID — we go by patient ID rather than name for HIPAA reasons. Their current status in the study. When their next visit is. And whether there's anything flagged on their record.
Sarah
UX Design
What statuses does a patient move through over the life of the study?
Brittany
Coordinator
Top-level: screened, enrolled, on treatment, completed, withdrawn. But there are sub-statuses too — a patient could be enrolled but on a treatment hold, or on treatment with a potential protocol deviation flag open against them. There's nuance there that matters.
Marcus
Product
For v1 we were thinking just the top-level statuses in the list view. The sub-status detail lives inside the patient record.
Part 3 — Patient record and actions
Sarah
UX Design
What does clicking into a patient record need to show you?
Brittany
Coordinator
Visit history — every visit they've had, what happened, any source document notes. Their upcoming visit schedule. And any open items: queries, flags, outstanding labs.
Sarah
UX Design
Is the record mostly read-only, or are there actions you'd need to take from there?
Brittany
Coordinator
You'd definitely take actions from there. Log a contact attempt — if I called a patient and they didn't answer I need to record that. And send a reminder — if a visit is coming up in a week I might want to send the patient a reminder.
Sarah
UX Design
When you say "send a reminder" — what does that actually look like from the patient's end?
Brittany
Coordinator
Right now I send an email. Sometimes I call. Some patients prefer texts. I guess whatever the system supports — I'm not really sure what's technically possible here.
Sarah
UX Design
Can coordinators schedule visits directly from within this tool?
Marcus
Product
That's a phase two feature. For v1 we want visibility only. Scheduling stays in the existing system.
Part 4 — Monitoring visit preparation
Sarah
UX Design
Let's talk about monitoring visit prep. You said you need to be ready before the CRA arrives — what does "ready" look like, concretely?
Brittany
Coordinator
I need to know which patients are going to be reviewed during the visit, which source documents are still outstanding, and whether any protocol deviations are documented but not yet submitted. The CRA will also want to see the delegation log is up to date, but that's usually something the PI handles.
Sarah
UX Design
What's the delegation log?
Brittany
Coordinator
It's a record of everyone on the study team and what they're each authorized to do. I can obtain consent and enter data, but I can't do medical assessments — that's the PI or sub-investigator. It needs to be current before every monitoring visit.
Marcus
Product
We're not building the delegation log into v1. But the tool does need to handle different user roles — a coordinator sees one view, the PI sees something different, and a CRA doing a monitoring visit should have a read-only view of the coordinator's perspective.
Sarah
UX Design
What specifically does the PI need to see that a coordinator doesn't?
Marcus
Product
We haven't fully worked that out yet. Probably medical assessments and any queries that require physician sign-off. We'll figure out the exact permission matrix in a follow-up session.
Part 5 — Users, access, and success
Sarah
UX Design
How many people would typically be using this at a single site?
Brittany
Coordinator
On a medium-sized study — two or three coordinators, one PI, maybe a sub-investigator. Then the CRA comes in roughly once a month for a monitoring visit.
Sarah
UX Design
Does this need to work on mobile?
Brittany
Coordinator
I'm almost always at my desk when I'm doing this work. But occasionally I'll check something at the site when I'm not at my desk — like if a patient shows up and I need to quickly pull their record to confirm a visit window.
Marcus
Product
Desktop-first, definitely. But it needs to be at least functional on a phone for those situations.
Sarah
UX Design
Last question — what does success look like? If this tool is working in six months, what's different about your day?
Brittany
Coordinator
I wouldn't start my day in email. I'd open the dashboard and in under two minutes I'd know what needs my attention today, what's coming up this week, and whether I'm ready for any upcoming monitoring visits. Right now I spend the first hour of every morning just figuring out where I stand. That hour is completely wasted.
After you run the Skill
These are the gaps that were deliberately left open in this transcript. Run the Skill first — then come back and compare. If the analysis surfaces these issues (and others), it's doing its job.
Undefined triggerThe red/amber/green priority system has no defined threshold — "we haven't fully worked out what triggers each level" leaves the designer with no criteria to design against.
Missing channelThe patient reminder action has no specified delivery mechanism — email, SMS, push, call — leaving a core interaction undefined.
Role permissionsPI vs. coordinator access is explicitly deferred ("we'll figure out the exact permission matrix in a follow-up session"). The CRA read-only scope is described loosely but not specified.
Undefined thresholdVisit window "closing" is never defined — how many days before the protocol deadline does a visit become urgent? This drives the entire priority system.
Sub-status designSub-statuses (treatment hold, protocol deviation open) are acknowledged as meaningful but deferred to "the patient record" without any design discussion.
Mobile scope"Functional on a phone" is never defined — which actions, which views, what level of fidelity.
No error statesNo discussion of what happens when data fails to load, a save fails, or the connection drops mid-session.
No empty statesA new study with no enrolled patients, a coordinator with no flagged items, a monitoring visit with no outstanding documents — none of these zero-data states are mentioned.
Contact attemptWhat fields are required when logging a contact attempt? What happens after multiple failed attempts? Is there an escalation path?
